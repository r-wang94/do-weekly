# Week 5: Seeds

---

Hello and welcome to Do Health Weekly. I'm Dr Robin Wang and this week, we're talking about seeds.

An acorn falls in autumn. It sits in the soil through winter. And for weeks, nothing happens. At least, nothing you can see. But underneath, it's already working. The first thing an acorn does is send a root straight down. Not up. Down. A taproot, pushing thirty centimetres into the earth before there's even a hint of a shoot above the surface. All the early energy goes underground. The root comes first. Always. The visible part is the last thing to arrive.

An oak tree won't produce its own acorns for twenty to thirty years. It doesn't hit its stride until fifty. And it can live for a thousand years. All of that, from something you could hold in the palm of your hand.

Now, the same thing is happening inside us. If we started strength training today, our bones would begin responding within minutes. The cells start signalling, recruiting, getting ready to rebuild. But a scan wouldn't show a thing for six months to a year. For women over forty, meaningful change in bone density takes about a year of consistent work. A whole year. We could be transforming ourselves at the deepest structural level and have absolutely no evidence of it. Our body is sending roots down, just like the acorn. We just can't see them yet.

And I think that's where the frustration lives. Not in the doing. The doing is fine. We can go for the walk. We can cook the meal. We can get to bed earlier. The hard part is the silence afterwards. The scales don't move. The energy doesn't feel different. And that voice starts up. Is this even working? Am I just wasting my effort here?

We've all had that moment. Three weeks in, four weeks in, doing everything we said we would, and nothing feels different. Sometimes, it's not the effort that breaks us. It's the absence of proof.

But here's what we don't get told. That silence isn't emptiness. It's the root going down. The body doesn't change in real time. It changes underneath, quietly, in places we can't measure yet, and the surface catches up later. The absence of visible progress isn't evidence that nothing is happening. It's evidence that the work is still underground.

And this is the part I think really matters. There's a detail about seeds that I can't stop thinking about. If you dig up a seed to check whether it's growing, you kill it. The checking is what stops the growth. Think about that for a second. The very act of pulling it up to look, of needing to see proof, is the thing that destroys what was already working.

We do this to ourselves all the time. We weigh ourselves every morning. We check the mirror. We ask am I there yet, am I there yet, am I there yet. And every time the answer comes back not yet, it doesn't feel like patience. It feels like failure. So we stop. Not because the process wasn't working. Because we dug it up too soon.

What if we just didn't? What if we just planted the walk, the early night, the Wednesday meal we actually cooked from scratch? And left it in the ground. Not forever. Just long enough for the roots to take hold.

The progress is real. The walks counted. The early nights counted. The fact that nothing looks different yet doesn't mean nothing is different. It means we're in the part that requires trust. And trust isn't passive. It's not sitting around hoping. It's planting again tomorrow even when yesterday's seeds haven't shown a thing.

We don't need more evidence that it's working. We need less digging.

So this week, instead of asking what's changed, ask a different question. What have I been planting that I stopped giving myself credit for?

You don't need to do anything new this week. You just need to see what you're already doing differently.

The root comes first. Always. And it's already going down.

So this week... is the week of seeds.

Thanks for being here. I'll talk to you next week.
