# Week 5: Capacity

---

Hello and welcome to Do Health Weekly. I'm Dr Robin Wang and this week, we're talking about capacity.

A friend told me about a children's book called The Giving Tree by Shel Silverstein. A boy loves a tree. The tree loves him back. When he's young, they play together. But as he grows up, he keeps coming back asking for more. He needs money, so the tree gives him her apples to sell. He needs a house, so she gives her branches. He needs a boat, so she gives her trunk.

Every time, the same line. "And the tree was happy."

Until there's nothing left but a stump.

It's supposed to be a story about generosity. But I think it's about what happens when the giving never stops. And the hardest part? The tree never noticed it happening. Because each time, it was just one thing. Just the apples. Just the branches. Just one more yes.

The job. The kids. The admin. The friend who's going through something. The parent who needs help with the thing. Each one, completely reasonable. And each time, we barely notice what we gave away.

Most of us have been running on nothing so long we've forgotten what it feels like to have something left.

And the strange part isn't that we end up there. Everyone does. The strange part is what we do once we're in it.

Everything in your week does one of two things. It either drains you or it charges you. And when you're running low, you'd think you'd protect the charges. But we do the opposite.

We keep every drain running. The emails. The obligations. Saying yes to things we don't have the energy for. And the things that would actually charge us back up? The Thursday evening swim gets cancelled. Your friend doesn't get called back. The early night becomes another late one, because by the time everyone else is sorted, the evening is already gone.

We keep the drains. We cut the charges.

Now. Why?

We cut the charges because they never feel essential. The swim feels like a nice-to-have. Calling your friend feels like something you'll get round to when things calm down. The early night feels like a luxury you haven't quite earned.

And the thing is, most of what drains us genuinely matters. The emails need answering. Your mum does need help. But so many of the things that make it into the essential column are the ones that are for someone else. Everything for you goes to the bottom of the list. And, often, the bottom of the list never comes.

So we end up with a week full of things that matter, and no room for the one thing that makes us able to do any of them to the full.

And it goes deeper than that.

Being exhausted can start to feel like proof you're doing it right. "I'm so busy." "I'm absolutely shattered." And everyone nods, because that's what a committed person sounds like. We wear our depletion like a badge. Nobody pinned it on us. It just started to feel, somewhere along the way, like pouring from empty was the price of being needed.

But tiredness is not a personality. And emptiness is not a virtue.

When Jacinda Ardern stepped down as Prime Minister of New Zealand, she said, "I no longer have enough in the tank to do it justice." Not a scandal. Not a crisis. The most powerful person in the country chose to stop before empty.

What comes out when you keep giving from nothing isn't nothing. It's a worse version of everything. Less patient with your kids. Less present when someone you love actually needs you there. Less kind to yourself when things go wrong. Not because you've stopped caring. Because there's genuinely nothing left to draw from.

But when you keep the charge. When the swim happens. When the walk isn't the thing that gets cancelled. Something shifts.

It's not dramatic. You don't wake up transformed. But the 3pm slump doesn't hit the same way. You make dinner and it's just dinner, not one more thing to survive. You laugh at something stupid and it's real, not performed.

You've had days like that. Maybe not recently. But you know what they feel like. You're not patient because you're trying to be. You're patient because there's something left to give. You actually want to hear about their day. The friend who calls gets the real you, not the version that's just trying to get through the conversation.

That's not a different person. That's you with something in the tank. Not just after a holiday, or a spa day. Just enough charge that what you give actually has you in it.

So this week, I'm not asking you to do more. I'm asking you to protect one charge. One. The thing you were about to cancel, postpone, or quietly talk yourself out of. The swim. The call. The walk before the house wakes up. Twenty minutes with a closed door and no one else's problems.

Not because it's a luxury you need to earn. Because with it, everything that comes out of you is just that little bit brighter.

Sometimes, the most selfless thing you can do is make sure there's something left to give.

So this week... is the week of capacity.

Thanks for being here. I'll talk to you next week.
